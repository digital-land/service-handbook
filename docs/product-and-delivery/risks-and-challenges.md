## Data coverage

Most data consumers are already piecing together datasets from across the planning data landscape, so the biggest driver of value from the platform is nation-scale data available in one place. We've validated this with data consumers, and it's one of the driving forces behind the national standard for planning applications and decisions.

The platform provides a wide range of datasets published at national scale currently, and any datasets collected from local sources need to be as deep. This best supports two key user groups: property developers (directly or via PropTech) and central government analysts.

On the supply side, the goal is to collect and index planning data at national scale as quickly as possible. This means understanding deeply the blockers data providers face and what might enable them to provide high-quality data more easily. It's a growth problem. ([Extract](https://github.com/digital-land/digital-land/issues/360) is an example of trying to solve an aspect of the growth problem.)

Working closely with data providers to keep data flowing is important.

## Data quality and freshness

Poor quality data isn't usable. One data point is useful, but more data over time tells a deeper story. 

Our data providers are mostly local authorities, with established processes and their own needs for the data, and many LPAs are resource-constrained. This makes it harder to encourage them to maintain the data and keep it up-to-date. This is where providing value back to LPAs will help: if they find our feedback and services valuable for managing their own data, for their own needs, it will also benefit our data consumers. 

This means our data operations and internal tools need to be excellent.
